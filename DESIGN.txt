ADVERSARY — Master Design & Development Plan
Vision Statement
Adversary is a single-screen platformer in the tradition of Donkey Kong, filtered through the 
death mechanics of Dark Souls and the gothic atmosphere of early Castlevania, with subtle 
references to Dark Souls lore woven into its four levels. It targets the aesthetic, feel, and sound 
of a lost 1984-1986 NES cartridge. Built in Phaser.js for browser play, architected from day one 
for Electron packaging and eventual Steam distribution.

Hit Reaction / Knockback
When the player takes damage, they are knocked backward and upward in the air — identical in 
feel to Castlevania 1. This is not merely cosmetic: the knockback can send the player off 
platforms, into hazards, or into enemies, making positioning and terrain awareness critical. 
Knockback direction is always away from the damage source. The player has brief invincibility 
frames after being hit (i-frames) to prevent chain damage. Enemies have their own lighter 
knockback reactions when struck.
 
Death & Soul Mechanic
1.	Player dies ? soul orb spawns at last safe ground position before death (or nearest 
reachable safe location if the player died falling off-screen)
2.	Player respawns at stage start with 1 HP, no XP bonuses active, gear retained
3.	Player reaches soul ? XP and level bonuses restored, game continues
4.	Player dies again before reaching soul ? GAME OVER
5.	Soul is visible on screen as a distinct glowing orb with a pulse animation
6.	If soul is off-screen, it should be placed back to nearest safe reachable location.
 
The Phantom — Anti-Grinding Mechanic
Inspired by the ghost in the original Spelunker, a Phantom enemy will emerge from off-screen if 
the player remains on a single screen section too long without progressing. The Phantom is 
completely invincible — it cannot be damaged or destroyed by any means. It moves slowly but 
relentlessly toward the player, ignoring platforms and walls, passing through all geometry. It 
deals heavy damage on contact and cannot be outrun indefinitely on a single screen. The only 
way to make the Phantom retreat is to advance to a new section of the level or reach the 
checkpoint.
This mechanic prevents cheap XP grinding by making stalling dangerous without imposing a 
hard time limit. The Phantom's arrival is telegraphed by a distinct audio cue and a brief visual 
warning so the player always has a moment to react. On later loops, the Phantom's trigger timer 
shortens and its movement speed increases slightly. Not sure if this mechanic should come into 
play on the boss stage, probably not.

?	Rusted Ring — slight speed boost
?	Hawk Ring — increases special attack range
?	Chloranthy Ring — faster mana regen
?	Covetous Ring — increased drop rates

Breakable Objects: Barrels, crates, coffins appear throughout all levels. Drop rates are lower

Chests & Mimics (Post-Loop 1)
Beginning after the first successful loop, chests begin appearing in the world. They are rare and 
their appearance is always a small reward moment.
Normal Chests appear occasionally after defeating enemies or after pulling off impressive 
jumps or kills (the game tracks these contextually). They have a higher-than-normal chance of 
dropping equipable items or bonus XP, making them meaningfully better than standard enemy 
drops.
Mimics look identical to normal chests. The tell, for observant players, is subtle — a slight 
animation difference or a faint sound cue. If the player attacks a mimic before touching it, it 
takes damage like a normal enemy. If the player touches a mimic without attacking first, it 
delivers a heavy damage hit and transforms — growing legs and becoming a fast, aggressive, 
difficult enemy. Defeating a mimic guarantees a rare or better drop. Mimic frequency increases 
with each loop.

Save System Architecture (SaveManager)
SaveManager
  .saveHighScores(scores)
  .loadHighScores()
  .saveSettings(settings)
  .loadSettings()
In browser: uses localStorage. In Electron build: uses Steam Cloud via greenworks or falls back 
to local file. Game code never calls localStorage directly — always through SaveManager — 
making the swap to Steam Cloud clean and low-risk.
 
Asset Integration Plan
All assets loaded via Phaser's asset loader from an /assets directory. Sprite sheets use 
texture atlases (JSON + PNG). The game ships with clearly named placeholder colored 
rectangles for all sprite slots so development can proceed fully before final art is ready.
Sprite categories needed:
?	Player: idle, walk, jump, duck, attack, air attack, plunge, hurt/knockback, death, soul orb
?	Per enemy: idle, walk, attack, death
?	Chest: closed, opening, Mimic idle, Mimic reveal, Mimic walk, Mimic attack
?	Phantom: idle, move (semi-transparent, ghostly)
?	Environment: tileset per level (floor, wall, platform, ladder, background, breakables)
?	UI: HUD elements, NES-style bitmap font, menu screens, heart/mana icons
?	Effects: projectiles, impacts, soul orb glow, level up flash, Phantom warning overlay
NES Palette Constraint: Limit to the 54 NES hardware colors. Max 4 colors per background 
tile, max 3 colors + transparency per sprite. This is the single most important visual constraint 
for authenticity. Exact hex palette specs will be provided when writing asset generation prompts.

Prompt Writing for Asset Generation
When ready, detailed prompts will be written for each sprite sheet and background tileset — 
specifying exact pixel dimensions (16x16 tiles, 16x32 player sprite), NES color palette hex 
values, animation frame counts, and per-level visual direction. These can target Figma AI tools, 
Midjourney with pixel art settings, or dedicated pixel art tools like Aseprite.
